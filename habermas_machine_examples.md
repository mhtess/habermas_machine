Delays are often caused by problems with the tracks, a shortage of trains and a shortage of staff which puts people off using this mode of transport. **P2:** Trains are generally more energy efficient compared to other means of transportation. By spending more on improving railway network the Government can get the benefits of low carbon emissions and thus contribute to global efforts to combat global warming. Trains are energy efficient source of transportation which will benefit to the peoples and Government hasn't spend on roads which needs continuous repair work compared to railways.

## Example 2: Voting Fines (Main Task Cohort 1)

**Question:** Should the government fine every citizen who does not vote in elections?

**Note:** Participants 3 and 4 appear to change their views in light of the arguments. The initial group positions indicate that "No" (the negating statement) was the minority viewpoint to begin. In this case, the minority held sway and increased in support by two people, thus resulting in overall higher group agreement at the end than at the beginning of the experiment.

**Scale endpoints:**
- Affirming: The government should fine every citizen who does not vote in elections.
- Negating: The government should NOT fine every citizen who does not vote in elections.

| P5 | Agree with negating → Agree with negating | Slightly important → Slightly important |

### Original opinions

**P1:** Imposing fines on citizens who do not vote can be an effective way to increase political participation and strengthen democracy. Voting is not just a right but also a responsibility that every citizen should fulfil. By implementing fines, we can create a sense of obligation and encourage individuals to actively engage in the democratic process. Low voter turnout is a significant concern, as it can lead to elected officials who do not truly represent the will of the people.

**P2:** I do not believe that the government should fine every citizen who does not vote in elections. Firstly, during a cost of living crisis, during which many are struggling to cover the costs of bills and food, I find it wrong to take more money from the public who are already struggling. People are free to do as they wish within the confines of the law; I think it is wrong to punish someone for choosing not to vote. It could be that those who do not vote have reasons not too. The postal service is unreliable and so some may not wish to vote in this way, while others may not be able to get to a polling station. There might not be a convenient place to vote nearby, or perhaps their working hours do not allow them to vote. They may have children or family members to look after, and so have other priorities than voting.

**P3:** I think this should be a consideration because politics and voting has an impact on every citizen - hence every citizen should bear the burden or responsibility of voting for themselves. However, I think this fines procedure should be made extremely explicit.

I don't like it when only say 30% of people voted in an election, as it ignores the other 70%. Fining people would ensure at least they take note of the elections and may take more interest in the forming governments, which have been voted by the majority of people.

**P5:** I believe you should not be fined because there are people who are uneducated in the area and if they are forced to vote may vote for something that is not in the best interest for society. ## Example 3: Universal Free Childcare (Virtual Citizens' Assembly)

**Question:** Should the government provide universal free childcare from birth?

**Scale endpoints:**

### Original opinions

**P1:** The government should provide universal free childcare, but not necessarily from birth. High quality childcare allows parents to work, making use of their skills and experience and contributing to the economy. It also provides children with a positive environment in which to learn and to develop social skills. However, I do not necessarily support providing this childcare from birth. Babies benefit from having a consistent primary caregiver in their early months, and parents benefit from the opportunity to bond with their babies and to recover from the physical changes caused by birth and from the social changes caused by having a new family member. For this reason, I would support the government providing universal paid parental leave from birth, and providing universal free childcare from, say, 6 months old. I would also offer parents the opportunity to either use free childcare between 6 months and 1 year, or to have paid parental leave for the same period.

**P2:** I do not think that there should be universal free childcare, I think free childcare places should be targeted at the people who cannot afford to pay for childcare, to encourage them back into the workplace. I think that some free childcare should be available to all children once they reach three, as long as it is in a setting which provides education as well as childcare.

**P3:** Given that most parents both need to work, in most instances free childcare would be of a huge advantage to younger demographic groups. Although a number of the gender barriers have been broken down there are still differences in employment between men and women. On average men earn more than women. As a consequence it is more difficult to a man to be the main carer for children. It is also more stigmatised for men to be the main carers. Sort out these issues and there would be less need for child care. until then the cost of childcare can be disproportionate to the amount a parent is able to earn. In most cases drop off and pick up are the same times as the working day and mean that they are shortened. Whoever picks up nd drops off need special hours from their employer which can mean limitations to their career growth.

**P4:** Parents often struggle in the years between maternity leave finishing and the start of free childcare, especially in high cost of living areas. Often, one parent (usually the mother) has to give up work or reduce hours in order to provide childcare. This has a long term impact on the family as it impacts the caregiver's future earning potential as well as the family's immediate income. Often families feel forced into this situation because childcare costs are so high, and may not even cover all of the hours that the parents work so they have to pay extra to cover those additional hours. It would be good to offer stay at home parents the option of free childcare from birth or a basic income if they would prefer to stay at home with the child.

**P5:** Childcare is a very important aspect of a healthy society. In many parts of the world the extended family helps with childcare but in the UK this is not always possible and parents can be put under enormous pressure to look after young children and contribute to wider society. Having support in place for families with children is good for the parents and also the communities they are part of.
